# Discovery Framework

A structured approach to discovery using MEDDIC. Use this as a guide, not a script — great discovery feels like a conversation, not an interrogation.

---

## MEDDIC Overview

| Letter | Stands for | What you're trying to learn |
|--------|-----------|---------------------------|
| **M** | Metrics | What does success look like, quantified? |
| **E** | Economic Buyer | Who controls the budget and final decision? |
| **D** | Decision Criteria | What criteria will they use to make the decision? |
| **D** | Decision Process | What does the buying process look like, step by step? |
| **I** | Identify Pain | What's the specific, quantifiable pain driving urgency? |
| **C** | Champion | Who internally is selling this for us when we're not in the room? |

**A deal with weak MEDDIC is a deal at risk.** If you can't answer every letter, you have discovery gaps.

---

## M — Metrics

**Goal**: Quantify the impact of the problem and the value of solving it.

Opening questions:
- "What does success look like for you in the first 90 days? First year?"
- "How are you currently measuring [the problem area]?"
- "If this went perfectly, what would the numbers look like?"

Probing questions:
- "You mentioned [X] is a problem — how much time/money/pipeline does that cost you per [week/quarter]?"
- "What's the cost of not solving this in the next 6 months?"
- "If we could [solve problem], what would that be worth to the business?"

**What good looks like**: You can fill out an ROI model with their actual numbers.

---

## E — Economic Buyer

**Goal**: Identify who controls budget and has final authority — and get in front of them.

Opening questions:
- "Who else is involved in evaluating solutions like this?"
- "Who signs contracts of this size at [Company]?"
- "Is there a budget already set aside for this, or does it need to be approved?"

Navigating to the EB:
- "Given the ROI we've been building together, would it make sense to include [EB name] in our next conversation?"
- "What does [EB name] care most about when evaluating investments like this?"

**Red flag**: You've never met the Economic Buyer before legal/negotiation. Fix this immediately.

---

## D — Decision Criteria

**Goal**: Understand exactly what they'll use to compare you against alternatives.

Opening questions:
- "If you were writing the evaluation scorecard, what would be on it?"
- "What does your team need to see before they feel confident recommending a vendor?"
- "Are there any hard requirements that would disqualify a solution?"

Probing questions:
- "You mentioned [criteria] — how important is that relative to [other criteria]?"
- "What would 'winning on [criteria]' look like for you?"

**Influencing criteria**:
- "A lot of our customers in your situation find [our strength] to be the biggest differentiator — is that something that matters to your team?"

---

## D — Decision Process

**Goal**: Map every step between here and signed contract, and know who's involved at each step.

Opening questions:
- "Can you walk me through how you typically make a decision like this?"
- "Once you decide on a vendor, what happens next? What does the legal and procurement process look like?"
- "What are the key milestones between now and go-live?"

Probing questions:
- "Is there a security or IT review? Who runs that?"
- "Does legal need to review the contract? How long does that typically take?"
- "Is there a board/exec approval required for a commitment of this size?"

**What good looks like**: You can build a mutual close plan with specific dates and owners.

---

## I — Identify Pain

**Goal**: Find the specific, urgent, quantifiable pain. Pain drives urgency; urgency drives deals.

Opening questions:
- "What's prompting you to look at solutions like ours now?"
- "What happens if you don't solve this in the next 6 months?"
- "What's the biggest challenge your team faces today with [area]?"

Deepening pain:
- "How long has this been a problem?"
- "What have you tried before? Why didn't it work?"
- "Who else feels this pain most acutely on your team?"

**The 'so what' test**: Keep asking "so what does that mean for the business?" until the answer is a number or an executive-level consequence.

---

## C — Champion

**Goal**: Identify and develop someone who will sell for you when you're not in the room.

Signs of a true champion:
- Has access to the Economic Buyer
- Shares internal information freely (org charts, political dynamics, competing priorities)
- Has personally experienced the pain you solve
- Has something to gain from this deal succeeding

Champion development questions:
- "What would this mean for you personally if it goes well?"
- "Who else on your team is feeling this pain? Could I talk to them?"
- "How can I help you make the case internally?"

**Testing your champion**:
- Ask them to set up a meeting with the EB. A real champion will do it.
- Ask for internal documents (current-state data, org chart). A real champion will share.
- Ask what's happening in their next internal planning session around this. A real champion will tell you.

---

## Discovery Anti-Patterns (What Not To Do)

- **Pitching before discovering**: Never demo before you've done at least 45 min of real discovery
- **Surface-level pain**: "We want to improve efficiency" is not a pain point. Push to the specific consequence.
- **Skipping the EB**: If you've never talked to the person who signs the check, you're at risk
- **No mutual close plan**: If you don't have an agreed-upon path to close, you don't have a deal
- **Champion ≠ Sponsor**: Your day-to-day contact is not automatically your champion. Test it.

---

## Discovery Call Structure (60 min)

| Time | Segment |
|------|---------|
| 0-5 min | Set agenda, establish rapport |
| 5-20 min | Their world — business context, current state, what prompted this |
| 20-40 min | Pain deep-dive — MEDDIC probing |
| 40-50 min | High-level solution fit — connect pain to your capabilities |
| 50-60 min | Next steps — confirm mutual interest, set next meeting |

**Rule**: If you're demoing in a discovery call, you're doing it wrong.
