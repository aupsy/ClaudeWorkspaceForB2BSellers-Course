# Objection Handling

Common objections and proven responses. Adapt to your product and customer context.

**Principle**: Objections are not rejections. They're unresolved concerns. Your job is to understand the concern, validate it, and resolve it — not bulldoze it.

**Framework (LAER)**:
1. **Listen** — Let them finish. Don't interrupt.
2. **Acknowledge** — "That's a fair concern, a lot of our customers have felt the same way."
3. **Explore** — Ask questions to understand the real concern underneath. "What specifically worries you about X?"
4. **Respond** — Answer with data, proof, or a reframe. Then confirm: "Does that address your concern?"

---

## Price / Budget Objections

### "It's too expensive."

Explore first:
- "Compared to what — our competitors, your budget, or the cost of the problem?"
- "What budget range were you expecting to be in?"

Then respond based on what you learn:
- **vs. competitors**: "Let's look at total cost of ownership — [competitor] often requires [add-ons] to match what we include."
- **vs. budget**: "If the ROI gets you to [X] in [timeframe], does the investment still feel too expensive?"
- **vs. problem cost**: "You mentioned [problem] costs you [amount] per quarter. At [our price], we'd pay for ourselves in [timeframe]."

### "We don't have budget right now."

- "When does your budget cycle reset?"
- "If we could show strong enough ROI, could budget be found this quarter?" (Tests whether it's real)
- "Would it be worth building the business case together so you're ready when Q[X] budget opens?"

### "We need a lower price."

- "I want to get this done — help me understand what's driving the number. Is it total spend, or is it something else?"
- If you have flexibility: "There are a few things I can look at — multi-year commitment, reduced seat count, or phased rollout. Which would be most useful?"
- If you don't: "I'm not able to change the price without a business reason I can bring to my manager. What would make this a clear yes?"

## Timing Objections

### "We're not ready to buy right now / We want to wait until [Q2 / after we hire a CRO / etc.]"

Explore:
- "What needs to happen before you'd feel ready?"
- "What's the risk if you wait? What does [problem] cost you in the meantime?"

Then respond:
- "Given your timeline for [stated goal], if we start onboarding in [month], you'd have [capability] in place before [their deadline]. If we wait until [Q2], you're looking at [Q3] for impact — does that timing still work for you?"

### "Let's revisit in 6 months."

- "I can do that. What would need to be true in 6 months for this to move forward?"
- "What would change between now and then that you'd need to see?"
- (If nothing would change): "If the situation is the same in 6 months, I'm not sure we'd be in a better position than we are today. What would make today a better time?"

## Competitive Objections

### "We're also looking at [Competitor]."

- "Good to know — have you had a chance to go deep on [specific capability that's our strength]? That's often where we see the sharpest difference."
- "What does [Competitor] do well for you? What are the things that still have you evaluating?"

### "We've heard [Competitor] is better at [X]."

- "What specifically did they show you there?"
- If it's wrong: "That's interesting — [correct framing with specific example]."
- If it's right: "They do [X] well. Where we outperform is [Y and Z]. For your use case, which matters more?"

### "We're going to go with [Competitor]."

- "I respect that decision — can I ask what tipped it in their favor?"
- This is intelligence for your next deal. Never burn the bridge.
- If there's still time: "Is there anything we could do or show you in the next [X days] that would reopen the evaluation?"

## Status Quo / Change Management Objections

### "We've always done it this way / We're fine with our current process."

- "What does the current process cost you in time/money/headcount?"
- "What would it take for 'fine' to become 'great'?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about the current approach, what would it be?"

### "Change is hard. Our team won't adopt it."

- "That's one of the biggest things we focus on — adoption. Can I show you what our onboarding looks like?"
- "What's worked in the past when you've rolled out new tools?"
- "What would make your team excited about this rather than resistant?"

## Procurement / Process Objections

### "Our procurement process is really slow."

- "What's the typical timeline once we have a signed agreement from your side?"
- "Is there anything I can do to help speed that up — standard MSA, security documentation, anything from our side that's usually the bottleneck?"

### "Legal needs to review our contract."

- "Of course — we have standard terms, or we can start from yours. What's the preferred starting point?"
- "How long does legal review typically take? I want to make sure we're accounting for that in the timeline."

### "We need your security questionnaire filled out."

- "Happy to do that. Who should I send it to? And do you have a template, or would our SOC 2 documentation be the right starting point?"

## The Objection You Should Never Get (But Sometimes Do)

### "We don't see the value."

If you get here, you failed discovery. Don't try to pitch your way out — go back to basics:

- "Tell me more — what were you expecting to see that you didn't?"
- "What's the most important problem you're trying to solve? Let me make sure I understand it correctly."
- Then: re-do the value conversation with their actual pain, not generic benefits.
