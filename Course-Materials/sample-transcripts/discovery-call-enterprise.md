# Discovery Call Transcript
**Account**: Meridian Industrial Group
**Date**: 2026-03-10
**Duration**: 47 minutes
**Participants**:
- Alex Chen (Rep, Your Company)
- Sarah Kowalski (VP of Sales, Meridian Industrial Group)
- Dan Wu (Director of Sales Operations, Meridian Industrial Group — joined at minute 12)

*[This is a sample transcript for course practice. Company and individuals are fictional.]*

---

**[0:00]**
**Alex**: Sarah, thanks for making time. I know you're in the middle of a busy quarter. I'll keep us focused. Can I ask — what made you take this meeting?

**Sarah**: Honestly, one of my peers at Hadley Manufacturing mentioned your name at a conference last month. She said she'd cut her AE ramp time significantly. That caught my attention because we have a major hiring wave coming and I'm worried about the same thing.

**Alex**: What does your ramp situation look like right now?

**Sarah**: It's bad. Our average time to first deal is about eight to nine months for an enterprise AE. We're a complex sale — a lot of product knowledge required, and our buyers are sophisticated. But eight months is painful. We lose productivity and we lose people. A lot of reps quit before they ever really get going.

**Alex**: What's the target? If you could wave a wand, where would you want that number?

**Sarah**: Four to five months would be transformational for us. Six would be a big improvement. Eight to nine is just not acceptable anymore.

**Alex**: And you mentioned a hiring wave — what does the pipeline look like there?

**Sarah**: We're planning to bring on eighteen new enterprise AEs in Q2. That's a big cohort. If we onboard them the way we've been doing it — which is basically just throwing them into the deep end and hoping the good ones swim — we're going to have a very rough second half.

**[12:00]**
*(Dan Wu joins)*

**Sarah**: Sorry — Dan, glad you could join. Alex, Dan runs Sales Operations for us. He's the one who actually has to deal with the consequences of our onboarding process.

**Dan**: *(slightly dry)* That's one way to put it.

**Alex**: Dan, good to meet you. Sarah was just walking me through the ramp situation. Eight to nine months to first deal. Eighteen AEs coming in Q2. What does that look like from your side?

**Dan**: It looks like a support ticket backlog and a lot of manager hand-holding. Sales managers spend probably thirty percent of their time coaching new reps on product questions they should be able to answer themselves. That's time they're not spending on pipeline or customer issues.

**Alex**: Is there a way to quantify the impact? Like, if we're losing eight months of productivity per rep, what does that cost you?

**Sarah**: We've actually tried to work this out. *(pause)* Our average AE has a six hundred thousand dollar quota. If they're only operating at half capacity for their first eight months — which is generous — that's roughly two hundred thousand dollars in lost potential per rep. Across last year's cohort of eleven new AEs, we estimate we left about two point one million on the table.

**Alex**: Two point one million. That's a number I want to make sure I capture correctly.

**Sarah**: Yes. And that's a conservative estimate. It doesn't account for the reps who quit before they close anything, which is about one in four.

**[22:00]**
**Alex**: What does your current onboarding look like? Walk me through what a new AE experiences in their first ninety days.

**Sarah**: Week one is product training — mostly death by PowerPoint from our product team. Then they shadow some calls for two to three weeks. Then they're kind of... on their own. We have a sales playbook but honestly it's outdated and nobody reads it. After that, it's whatever their manager has capacity to give them.

**Dan**: The playbook was last updated eighteen months ago. It doesn't even mention two of our major product lines.

**Alex**: So there's no consistent, live context that a new rep can go to when they're in a deal and need an answer?

**Sarah**: No. They Slack their manager, or they ask a colleague. Which is fine if the manager is available and the colleague gives them the right answer. Neither of those is guaranteed.

**Alex**: What about competitive situations? How do your new reps handle a prospect who's evaluating you against a competitor?

**Sarah**: That's actually where we lose the most deals in the first six months. A new rep just doesn't have the instincts yet. They don't know how to position against — particularly against Highspot, which we see a lot. We looked at Highspot ourselves a couple years ago, actually, but decided not to go with them.

**Alex**: What made you choose not to go with them at the time?

**Sarah**: They were strong on content management but weak on the coaching and performance side. Our sales process needs both. But they've invested since then — I'm honestly not sure where they stand today.

*(Note: Alex does not follow up on this. No question about current Highspot stance in market, no competitive landmine planted.)*

**[32:00]**
**Alex**: Let me describe what we do and you can tell me if it resonates. *(describes solution — personalized just-in-time coaching, AI-powered deal guidance, knowledge base that stays current automatically, competitive battle cards surfaced in real time)*

**Sarah**: The real-time battle card piece is interesting. That's literally the thing I just described as our biggest gap for new reps.

**Dan**: How does the knowledge base stay current? Ours is a disaster because nobody has time to update it.

**Alex**: *(explains auto-updating mechanism)* The system pulls from your product team's materials, updates when you add new content, and surfaces the right information in the context of an active deal — so the rep sees the relevant piece when they need it, not a list of documents to search through.

**Dan**: How long does setup typically take?

**Alex**: For a company your size, typically six to eight weeks to full deployment. The first cohort to go through it sees results within thirty days of going live.

**Sarah**: *(pause)* I'll be honest — we're in the middle of a Salesforce migration right now. It's consuming a lot of IT and ops bandwidth. That might make timing tricky.

**Alex**: What's the migration timeline?

**Sarah**: We're hoping to be on the new instance by end of Q2. So May-ish.

**Alex**: So if we could structure a pilot that doesn't touch the migration — focused on your reps' workflow rather than Salesforce configuration — would that address the concern?

**Sarah**: *(thinking)* Maybe. I'd want to understand what the integration footprint looks like. Dan, can you stay on for a few more minutes after this call?

**Dan**: Sure.

**[42:00]**
**Alex**: Sarah, one thing I want to ask — we've talked about the cost of the problem. What's the value of solving it? If you cut ramp time from eight months to four, what does that mean for your business in concrete terms?

**Sarah**: *(thinks)* If these eighteen reps ramp in four months instead of eight, they each get an extra four months of full productivity this year. At sixty percent of quota... *(does mental math)* ...that's significant. If even half of them hit target in that extra time, you're talking multiple millions in revenue.

**Alex**: That math is worth quantifying precisely for a business case. I can help with that.

**Sarah**: *(nods)* If you can show me this cuts ramp time by even thirty percent, this is a no-brainer. Frankly if it gets us to six months from eight, I'd still do it.

**Alex**: Let me ask — what does a decision process look like for something like this? Who else would be involved?

**Sarah**: I'd want to bring in my CRO — Jennifer Park. She'd be the economic decision maker. And obviously Dan's team on the ops side. We'd want a demo that includes a use case specific to our industry and our sales process. Manufacturing is different from tech.

**Alex**: Absolutely. I can set that up. What's the best way to get something on Jennifer's calendar — do you want to facilitate that, or should I reach out to her directly?

**Sarah**: I'll pull her in. Let me talk to her this week. I'd say give me ten days and I'll come back to you with availability. We'd want a demo in the next few weeks if the CRO conversation goes well.

**Alex**: Perfect. I'll send you a recap of today and some materials about the manufacturing use case. Is there anything specific Jennifer will want to see covered?

**Sarah**: She'll want to understand the ROI story clearly. And she'll want to know what implementation looks like — she's been burned by long deployments before.

**[47:00]**
**Alex**: Last question — is there anything you'd want me to know about Meridian or your team that would help me prepare a better demo?

**Sarah**: We're conservative buyers. We move carefully. But when we commit, we commit. Don't oversell — just show me the thing doing what it claims to do.

**Alex**: Understood. I appreciate the directness. I'll have a recap to you by end of day tomorrow. And I'll look forward to hearing from you about Jennifer's availability.

**Sarah**: Sounds good. Dan, stick around for a couple minutes?

*[End of call — 47 minutes]*
