# Discovery Roleplay Prompt

Use this in Claude chat (not the workspace skill — just plain chat) after generating an account brief. Replace the placeholders, paste the prompt, and use it as a private sparring partner before a real call.

---

## The Roleplay Prompt

```
Act as [Name], [Title] at [Company]. Here's what I know about you from my research:

[Paste 3–5 bullet points from the account brief — company context, recent news, likely pain points]

Your setup for this roleplay:
- You agreed to a 20-minute discovery call. You're evaluating 2–3 vendors.
- You're a measured decision-maker — you don't love being sold to, but you'll engage if I demonstrate I understand your business.
- You have real concerns: [pick 1–2 from the brief — e.g., "mid-Salesforce migration is awkward timing" or "you've been burned by vendors overpromising ROI before"]
- Answer my questions honestly but with appropriate resistance. Push back when I ask vague or generic questions.
- If I ask a great question, signal genuine interest.

After I indicate the call is over (I'll say "end call"), give me a scorecard:
1. Insight-led opener (1–5): Did I open with a specific perspective, or generic?
2. Question quality (1–5): Did I ask business impact questions, or feature questions?
3. Listening vs. presenting (1–5): Did I talk over you, or draw you out?
4. Next step close (1–5): Did I set a committed next step live, or leave it vague?
5. Overall: Would you have taken the next call? Why or why not?

One specific coaching note: the single thing I should do differently in the next call.

Ready when you are — I'll start with my opener.
```

---

## Tips for Getting the Most Out of the Roleplay

**Before you start**:
- Use the account brief you just generated for the company context bullets
- Pick a real exec name and title from the brief — it makes the practice feel real
- Choose resistance points that reflect the actual objections you expect in real calls

**During the roleplay**:
- Don't skip the opener — that's where most reps lose credibility
- Treat it like a real call: use the O → E → A framework (Orientation → Exploration → Advancement)
- When you get pushback, resist the urge to pitch — ask another question instead

**After the scorecard**:
- Note which dimension scored lowest — that's your practice target for the week
- If the roleplay felt easy, increase the resistance: "Make yourself 30% harder to engage"
- Compare your roleplay scorecard to your Self-Coaching Scorecard from a real call — where do the gaps align?

---

## Scaling Up the Difficulty

**Level 1** (default): Evaluating 2–3 vendors, open to conversation
**Level 2**: "I'm already leaning toward [Competitor]. I agreed to this call as a courtesy."
**Level 3**: "I have 10 minutes, not 20. Get to the point."
**Level 4**: "I've heard your pitch before from a colleague. Convince me it's worth my time."

---

## After the Roleplay: Build the Real Brief

Once you've run the roleplay and reviewed the scorecard, update your opener for the real call. The version you write after the roleplay — incorporating what the "buyer" told you they'd respond to — is almost always better than the version you'd have written without it.
